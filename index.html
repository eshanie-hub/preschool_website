Literature Survey

Emotional intelligence (EI) in early childhood plays a critical role in shaping cognitive development, learning ability, and psychological well-being. Low emotional intelligence, expressed as emotional dysfunctionality, negatively affects attention, memory, and educational outcomes [1]. Many children at this stage lack the verbal ability and emotional awareness to clearly express their internal emotional states, which can lead to internalized distress, withdrawal behaviors, and later emotional instability [2]. If emotional distress is not identified early, it may develop into long-term emotional, behavioral, and social difficulties, while emotional neglect can significantly hinder overall development [3], [4].

Emotions are closely linked to cognitive brain functions, particularly through the roles of the amygdala and hippocampus in emotional regulation and memory formation. Positive emotional states enhance attention and long-term memory, whereas negative emotions such as stress and anxiety impair information processing and learning [5]. Emotional dysfunctionality is especially critical among children exposed to trauma or unstable environments. Although many existing approaches rely on digital technologies, they raise concerns related to privacy, ethical data usage, and excessive screen exposure. Therefore, this study proposes a privacy-preserving, non-digital, and culturally appropriate approach to identify emotional dysfunctionality among Sri Lankan children aged 4–6 years.

Research Gap

  • Need for Explainable, Pedagogy-Centric Design
  • Bias in Modeling & Localization
  • Lack of Non-Invasive Emotion Recognition
  • Fragmented Developmental Assessments

Research Problem

Current educational tools for early childhood development are often fragmented, intrusive, and lack local context, creating a barrier to effective early intervention. Our research addresses four critical limitations in the field: the reliance on privacy-invasive emotion recognition, the lack of explainable, bilingual (Sinhala/English) structural handwriting assessment, the absence of adaptive, real-time conversational tools tailored for the specific linguistic patterns and accents of Sri Lankan children and lack of fine motor with attention assessments. We aim to bridge these gaps by developing a unified, explainable, and culturally relevant framework that supports holistic growth in motor, academic, and linguistic skills.

Research objectives

★

Emotion Identification through arts

The primary objective of this research is to analyze the correlation between a child's artistic expression and their internal psychological state by integrating color clustering data with qualitative ERC metrics. By establishing a data-driven framework for negativity labeling, the study aims to provide educators with a reliable methodology for interpreting non-verbal cues to better support a child's emotional well-being.

★

Memory Recall Identification through handwriting

The second objective of this study is to develop a system to assess preschool children's memory recall through handwriting tasks while distinguishing recall errors from general writing difficulties. The system will evaluate writing accuracy against given stimuli to identify recall patterns and cognitive gaps, and provide feedback to support early learning and development.

★

Identification of Language Skills

The third objective is a specialized conversational voice assistant designed to support Sri Lankan children aged 4–6 in learning English as a second language. The system integrates a fine-tuned Whisper model for accurate accent recognition, a custom-trained model for grammar correction, and a reinforcement learning mechanism that dynamically adapts conversation prompts to each child's proficiency. Furthermore, the web-based dashboard provides teachers and parents with real-time performance tracking and explainable AI-driven insights to effectively foster the child's language development.

★

Focus Level Identification through coloring & tracing tasks

The fourth objective is to measure the focus level of children aged 4 to 6 using coloring and tracing activity images through computer vision techniques. It evaluates task completeness, boundary adherence, coloring outside lines, and completion time, enabling accurate assessment of attention, concentration, and overall engagement in fine motor skill activities.

Methodology

The methodology employs a multi-dimensional approach to assess child development, beginning with Emotion Identification through the feature extraction of artwork and the application of Deep Learning (DL) models to the Emotion Regulation Checklist, further supported by explainable AI for transparency. Memory Recall Identification analyzes handwriting through preprocessing and segmentation, utilizing dual-language DL models and a comparison engine to generate specific recommendations. Focus Level Identification evaluates coloring and tracing activities alongside completion time and age-based metrics to predict developmental results, while Language Skills Identification uses a voice assistant and Reinforcement Learning (RL) to assess spoken utterances via Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) and grammar error detection. All predicted results are integrated into a centralized Dashboard for data visualization, allowing teachers to access combined assessment insights while all information is securely stored within a backend Database.
